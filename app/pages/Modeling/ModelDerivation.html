    • Overview

    In this section we provide a derivation of our model. We present the reasoning behind the reactions and the corresponding assumptions. We start by modeling the regulation of the pETDuet-1 plasmid which consists of one gene coding for the lac repressor and two lac regulated genes which constitute the respective pathway in our AND-logic. The biomolecular network is then presented for each pathway followed by the final GFP complementation.

    The pETDuet-1 is a plasmid vector designed for the co-expression of two target genes. It has a copy number of 40 and is a T7 promoter expression vector. Each cloning site is preceded by a T7 RNAP promoter, a lac operator and ribosome binding site. In addition, the vector carries the lacI gene for the constituitive expression of the lac repressor. An illustration is shown in figure 1. The target genes to be expressed, $\ce{A}$ and $\ce{B}$ are regulated by two lac operons denoted $\ce{OA}$ and $\ce{OB}$. A constituitive promoter expresses the lacI gene which in turn represses $\ce{OA}$ and $\ce{OB}$, inactivating the expression of $\ce{A}$ and $\ce{B}$. The addition of an inducer represses the repressor LacI, which in turn activates the previously repressed operons. This pathway enables a very simple genetic switch which can be utilied to control gene expression by the addition of an inducer.


    The entire casette is illustrated in figure 1. The pathway starts with an ITPG induction to yield NahR and the Estrogen Receptor-α Ligand Binding Domain with linker bound gfp10 and gfp11 fragments. As salicylate is added it binds to the NahR to start the expression of the first gfp1-9 fragment. Simulatenously, an estrogen analog binds to the Estrogen Receptor-α LBD which causes a conformational change to bring the complementary gfp fragments gfp10 and gfp11 to come into close proximity. This situation allows for the gfp1-9 fragment to complement with gfp10 and gfp11 and cause fluorescence.

    lac regulation

    The model of the lac regulation is based on the work of M. Stamatakis and N. V. Mantzaris [2] who used mass action and stochastic kinetic models to study the autoregulation and bistability of the lac operon. Here we apply their model on our pETDuet-1 plasmid. We modify it to fit for the two lac operators sharing the resources from one lacI gene. A list of the species is found in table 1.

    Starting with the transcriptional pathway of the lac repressor monomer $\ce{R}$, the transcription of it's mRNA $\ce{M_R}$ is modeled as a first order reaction from the lacI gene $\ce{O_R}$. The following translation of $\ce{R}$ is proportional to $\ce{M_R}$ and thus also of first order. Subsequently, the monomer $\ce{R}$ dimerizes into the active LacI repressor $R_2$.

    $$\ce{O_R ->[k_\text{MR}] O_R + R}\tag{1}$$ $$\ce{M_R ->[k_\text{R}] M_R + R}\tag{2}$$ $$\ce{2R <-->[k_\text{2R}][k_\text{-2R}] R_2}\tag{3}$$

    Following, $\ce{R_2}$ is able to interact with the lac operons denoted $\ce{O_N}$ and $\ce{O_E}$, regulating the expression NahR and ER-α-LBD respectively, to yield the corresponding repressed states $\ce{R_2O_N}$ and $\ce{R_2O_E}$. This is modeled as two equilibriums were the repressed operons can dissociate back into the repressor and free operon.

    $$\ce{R_2 + O_N <-->[k_r][k_{-r}] R_2O_N}\tag{4}$$ $$\ce{R_2 + O_E <-->[k_r][k_{-r}] R_2O_E}\tag{5}$$

    The induction of the repressed state proceeds via an addition of inducer $\ce{I}$ with the repressor. This is modeled as a third order reaction where the entities of I binds to $\ce{R_2}$. The reaction can take place between both free and operator bound repressors.

    $$\ce{2I + R_2 <-->[k_{dr1}][k_{-dr1}] I_2R_2}\tag{6}$$ $$\ce{2I + R_2O_N <-->[k_{dr2}][k_{-dr2}] I_2R_2 + O_N}\tag{7}$$ $$\ce{2I + R_2O_E <-->[k_{dr2}][k_{-dr2}] I_2R_2 + O_E}\tag{8}$$

    The inducer $\ce{I}$ is distinguished from the extracellular inducer $\ce{I_{ex}}$ and the fluxes are modeled as free diffusion.

    $$\ce{I_{ex} <-->[k_{dI}][k_{dI}] I}\tag{9}$$

    The transcriptions and translations are modeled as catalytic first order reaction. the mRNA:s are denoted $M_N$ and $M_E$ for NahR and ER-α LBD respectively. We do not choose to take into account for leakage transcription as it is considered a second order effect. The transcripts for NahR and ER-α-LBD are denoted $\ce{M_N}$ and $\ce{M_E}$ respectively. They code for the corresponding unfolded proteins $\ce{uN}$ and $\ce{uE}$ which then folds into the functioning form $\ce{N}$ and $\ce{E}$.

    $$\ce{O_N ->[k_\text{MN}] O_N + M_N}\tag{10}$$ $$\ce{O_E ->[k_\text{ME}] O_E + M_E}\tag{11}$$ $$\ce{M_N ->[k_\text{N}] M_N + \text{u}N}\tag{12}$$ $$\ce{M_E ->[k_\text{E}] M_E + \text{u}E}\tag{13}$$ $$\ce{\text{u}N ->[k_\text{fN}] N}\tag{14}$$ $$\ce{\text{u}E ->[k_\text{fE}] E}\tag{15}$$

    All degradations are modeled as first order reactions. It is assumed that all mRNA:s and proteins degrade with the exception of the operator bound repressor. Note that it is also assumed that the inducer does not decompose.

    $$\ce{M_R ->[\lambda_\text{MR}] \phi}\tag{16}$$ $$\ce{M_N ->[\lambda_\text{MN}] \phi}\tag{17}$$ $$\ce{M_E ->[\lambda_\text{ME}] \phi}\tag{18}$$ $$\ce{R ->[\lambda_\text{R}] \phi}\tag{19}$$ $$\ce{R_2 ->[\lambda_{\text{R}_2}] \phi}\tag{20}$$ $$\ce{\text{u}N ->[\lambda_\text{N}] \phi}\tag{21}$$ $$\ce{N ->[\lambda_\text{N}] \phi}\tag{22}$$ $$\ce{\text{u}E ->[\lambda_\text{E}] \phi}\tag{23}$$ $$\ce{E ->[\lambda_\text{E}] \phi}\tag{24}$$ $$\ce{I_2R_2 ->[\lambda_{\text{I}_2\text{R}_2}] 2I}\tag{25}$$

    NahR

    It has been shown that NahR binds to the Psal promoter as a transcription factor regardless of the presence of salicylate [1989]. The binding affinity has previously been thought to base unaffected of salicylate, but recent in vitro studies with surface plasmon resonance indicate that it in fact has an inhibinting impact on the binding to Psal [SPR]. There has also been indications that NahR binds as a tetramer to the promoter [1990]. However, recent studies has shown that NahR actually binds sequentially as a monomer to the promoter and then undergoes a multimerization on site [SPR]. The degree of multimerization is to our knowledge ambiguous as immobilized Psal has had up to eight bound monomers. In addition, we have not been able to find the salicylate binding mechanisms to the Psal-NahR complex. We do thus not know wether one salicylate has to binds to each NahR before transcription can occur or wether the activity is increased gradually with each intermediately bound solute. Neither do we know how the transcriptional activity changes with each intermediate NahR monomer bound to Psal. Assuming all intermediate steps exist and contribute to the transcription, the model would quickly increase in size and complexity, yielding a big parameter space. To be able to model this correctly, there is a need of a big amount of data as the amount of data increases exponentially with each extra parameter. As we only have a sparse amount of data, we choose to model the pathway in two steps, where the NahR, $\ce{N}$, binds to Psal in a second order reaction.     hER

    The human estrogen receptor ER-α is modeled using simple reaction schemes. The binding of the estrogen antagonist $\ce{E}$ onto the estrogen receptor alpha ligand binding domain $\ce{ER}$ is modeled as a second order reaction where the active estrogen receptor state $\ce{\text{a}ER}$ is formed. The active state can decompose into $\ce{E}$ and $\ce{ER}$, but since the equilibrium constant is strongly shifted towards the binding formation (see parameter estimation), we model it as an irreversible reaction.

    $$\ce{E + Es ->[k_{ae}] EE\text{s}}$$

    The entry of the antagonist $\ce{E}$ is modeled by assuming an extracellular unit $\ce{E_\text{ex}}$ which diffuses over the cell membrane. $$\ce{E\text{s}_\text{ex} <-->[k] Es}$$ It is assumed later that the amount of $\ce{E_\text{ex}}$ is constant and is thus set as a boundary condition.

    The degradations are all of first order. $$\ce{E -> \phi}$$ $$\ce{EE\text{s} -> \phi}$$
